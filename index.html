About the breed

The story of the Roman Tufted Goose begins in Italy, 365 B.C.E., where legend has it the honk of a goose saved the capitol from a midnight siege.

I have yet to determine if our flock has any heroic qualities, but my suspcion is that they largely lost in the genetic lottery.

They are plump, hissy, and have anarchist tendencies. Their plumage is entirely white with a tuft on top that resembles a little bird bicycle helmet. Their bills and feet are a vibrant orange and their eyes are blue. Ganders (males) have a thicker neck and a louder honk.

Overall, Roman Tufted Geese make excellent lawn mowers and mediocre friends.

How We Got Here

It started with adorable little ducklings that we love with all our hearts. Every day we would take our ducklings out on a walk around the yard to snack on peas, bugs, and anything else they might want to forage. As they continued to grow, we decided we wanted to give them free range, but we were worried about their safety.

Enter geese! (AKA viscous feathered guard dogs.) Geese will alert very loudly when there is danger and they’re particularly astute to aerial predators like hawks, owls, and helicopters. We also have high hopes that they might deter our little fluffy mutt from showing affection to the ducks by putting them in his mouth.

So far, they’re very mediocre employees. There have been no duck fatalities yet, so we’ll call it a conditional success.

Meet Our Flock

Our particular flock of geese has been absurdly inbred for at least six generations that we know of and though assured by the breeder that “there haven’t been any problems yet!” we’re documenting new concerns every day. (See: concerning behaviors)

That said, good entertainment is hard to come by in the pandemic, and they provide plenty of it!

Bruce

Bruce is easy to tell apart from the others because he’s rockin' a bro comb over. Still not sure where he gets his hair gel. Every time I ask him he hisses at me.

Gwen

The first in our flock, she is the smallest goose and spent a few weeks alone among the ducks asking, “What the heck kind of bird am I?” She has since found her flock, but still behaves as the liaison to the ducks. Gwen is a cousin to the other geese, who we think are all immediate siblings.

Lucy

A larger female goose who has a bright orange bill and feet like her brothers, but you can tell her apart from them by her smaller and more tamed tuft.

Big Beau Crackly

Named by my three-year-old nephew, Big Bo Crackly is the largest goose with the thickest neck. He also holds his head at a higher angle at all times to assert dominance. It’s all a front and he’s definitely the biggest baby of them all.

Upsides?

If we keep them until next spring, we’ll get to try goose eggs. I’ve never had a goose egg before, but I imagine it takes cracking only one of these to make a big, fluffy omelet.

There is also the option of hatching and selling baby geese.

You know what they say about inbreeding, seventh time’s the charm!
